## Introduction: The Vicious Cycle of Poor Forecasting

* **The Hook:** Start with the common perception—forecasting has a terrible reputation in corporate environments.
* **The Vicious Cycle:** Because people believe forecasts are inherently inaccurate, they invest very little effort into building them. This lack of rigor guarantees the forecast *will* be bad, thus reinforcing the original prejudice.
* **The Thesis:** Forecasting is undeniably difficult, but high-quality, actionable results are entirely achievable if you treat it as a structural engineering problem rather than a guessing game.



The art and science of forecasting has a terrible reputation. People don't talk about great forecasts or forecasters, but you do hear when the forecasts are wrong. Perhaps there are examples in the finance industry, where big bets are made based on forecast future outcomes, but even then can be seen as distasteful profiting on the misery of others - e,g, Michael Burry's prediction of the US sub-prime collapse as made famous in the film 'the Big Short'.



However, outside financial speculation, failure to invest in forecast quality sets off a vicious cycle of poor forecasting results that further reduces faith in the merit of forecasting altogether. In my first ever professional forecasting assignment, my manager explained how he had been to see a major agency due to wildly inaccurate forecasts they had produced. He discovered the analyst was using a ruler on a hand drawn chart to make his forecasts. My manager joked how they identified the problem was the ruler was too short and the problem was solved with a longer ruler. It was the first of many instances I have come across where forecasts were done superficially.



Furthermore, there can be serious consequences for getting forecasts wrong, such as business closures and jobs lost, or opportunities missed.



Positively, there are steps that can be taken to substantially improve forecast quality and accuracy. While forecasts will never be perfect, but it can be good enough to make well considered business decisions.



## Defining a Good Forecast



Accuracy alone is not the measure of a good forecast. A forecast can be correct through sheer luck and should not be given any merit for the good fortune. Likewise a forecast that is wrong is not necessarily a bad forecast. Two key factors in assessing forecast quality are:

1. Did the forecast incorporate everything that could reasonably have been anticipated at the time it was made? For example, the turmoil in the oil markets that followed the US and Israel's bombing of Iran and subsequent closure of the Strait of Hormuz could not have been easily anticipated prior to the bombing. While there was increasing internal unrest in the weeks prior to the military operation, the outcome would at best have been one of many possible outcomes and not necessarily the most likely.
2. Was the forecast unbiased? Typically this means outcomes both above and below the forecast equally likely to occur.  Another way to look at this is to say on average, over repeated forecasting cycles, the forecast is correct on average. I have seen a conservative approach used for price forecasting that was based on prices being above forecast 70% of the time, and below 30% of the time.
3. Is the variability around the forecast low? Setting aside any events that couldn't reasonably have been anticipated, we need the variance to be sufficiently low to make relevant decisions. The variance you should be willing to accept depends very much on what is at stake, with big and especially one off decisions requiring the most robust process to minimise variance.





## The Anatomy of a Bad Forecast: Bias and Blind Spots

* **Intentional Bias (The easy culprit):** Briefly touch on sandbagging (lowering targets to ensure bonuses) or hockey-stick projections (inflating numbers for investors).
* **Unintentional Bias (The silent killer):** This is the core issue. When a forecast relies on "management's best guess," it is fundamentally flawed. A collective group of smart people guessing is still just guessing, and it incorporates massive subconscious bias (e.g., anchoring, recency bias, or what Daniel Kahneman calls WYSIATI - "What You See Is All There Is").
* **The Target vs. Forecast Trap:** How companies fatally confuse a *target* (what we want to happen) with a *forecast* (what the math says is most probable).





## Structural Fix 1: The Power of Granular Decomposition

* **The Concept:** High-level (e.g., national) forecasts miss critical underlying currents. By breaking the forecast down into its atomic components (e.g., state-level, product-line level, or demographic cohorts), you capture different, sometimes offsetting, trends. Noise at the top level often resolves into clear signals at the granular level.
